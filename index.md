## About

I am an auditory and computational cognitive neuroscientist. My research asks
how the human brain makes sense of sound — how we hear speech, music, and the
acoustic world, and how the brain and body stay coordinated during social
interaction. I approach these questions by combining behavioral experiments,
neural recordings (EEG/MEG), signal processing, and machine learning.

I am a Research Affiliate at [New York University](https://wp.nyu.edu/poeppellab/)
and work at Google. I completed my PhD in Psychology, Neuroscience & Behaviour at
McMaster University with Laurel Trainor, my postdoctoral training at NYU with
David Poeppel, and my BS in Psychology at National Taiwan University. My work has
been recognized by the National Institutes of Health, the New York Academy of
Sciences, the Governor General of Canada, NSERC, and the Cognitive Neuroscience
Society, and has been featured in *Scientific American*, where I also write.
</section>

<section id="research" markdown="1">
## Research Interests

- **Speech and music** — the acoustic, perceptual, and neural features that tell
  speech and music apart, and what that (dis)similarity means for people with
  communication disorders.
- **Auditory perception and neural oscillations** — how the brain tracks rhythm,
  pitch, and temporal structure to predict what comes next.
- **Machine learning for sound and behavior** — interpretable models that
  classify audio and predict human experience from multimodal signals.
- **Interpersonal coordination** — how body motion reveals leadership, emotion,
  and connection during joint music performance and conversation.
</section>
